I approached Hendrick's Orbium with curiosity, knowing it represented the distillery's departure into quininated territory. This expression takes the familiar cucumber-rose foundation of standard Hendrick's and layers in wormwood, quinine, and additional botanicals to create something noticeably more bitter and herbaceous.

On the nose, I immediately noticed the shift in character. While faint cucumber still appears, the aroma leans harder into herbal and slightly medicinal notes—think gentian root and tonic water concentrate. There's a floral undertone, but it's muted compared to the original Hendrick's rose prominence. The first sip confirmed my suspicion: this gin wants to be understood on its own terms, not as a simple variation.

The quinine presence is unmistakable. It brings a dry, mouth-coating bitterness that some will find sophisticated and others challenging. I found myself appreciating how it completely reimagines the gin and tonic—the quininated spirit meeting quinine-rich tonic creates an almost redundant intensity that serious tonic enthusiasts might love but casual drinkers could find overwhelming. In a classic G&T, I had to dial back my usual tonic ratio significantly.

Where Orbium struggled for me was in more delicate applications. A Martinez felt unbalanced, with the wormwood notes competing rather than complementing the vermouth. A Southside lost its refreshing character under the weight of the botanical bitterness. This gin performs best when you either feature it minimally diluted or pair it with equally assertive ingredients.

Neat or with just a splash of soda, I could appreciate the craftsmanship more clearly. The texture is smooth, the integration of botanicals feels intentional rather than haphazard, and there's genuine complexity to explore. I noticed lotus blossom character emerging in the mid-palate, adding a subtle sweetness that prevents the profile from becoming one-dimensionally bitter.

The finish is where opinions will divide. It's long and drying, with that quinine note persisting well after swallowing. I found it interesting rather than pleasant on its own, though in the right cocktail context it provided a satisfying backbone.

For value, I had to consider this as a specialty bottle rather than an everyday sipper. The price sits above standard Hendrick's, positioning Orbium as an occasional exploration rather than a staple. I wouldn't recommend it as someone's only gin—the flavor profile is too specific. But for those building a collection or seeking something distinctly different for particular cocktail applications, it offers a legitimate point of difference.

Ultimately, Hendrick's Orbium succeeds as an interesting experiment in quininated gin, though it demands the right context and palate to shine. I respect what it's trying to do more than I reach for it regularly.
