## A Bitter Botanical Experiment Worth Exploring

I approached Hendrick's Orbium with curiosity about how the quininated botanical profile would differentiate it from the cucumber-rose signature of standard Hendrick's. The result is a gin that ventures confidently into bitter territory, creating something genuinely distinct rather than simply iterative.

On the nose, I immediately detected the floral notes that Hendrick's is known for, but they're accompanied by a more herbal, slightly medicinal quality. The wormwood and quinine additions announce themselves clearly—this isn't a subtle variation. Tasting it neat reveals layers of complexity: the familiar juniper backbone, cucumber coolness, and rose petal sweetness are all present, but they're now framed by a pronounced bitterness that lingers on the palate.

Where Orbium truly excels is in its synergy with tonic water. Since both the gin and tonic contain quinine, there's a natural harmony that creates a more integrated drinking experience than typical gin and tonics. I found the bitterness actually balanced rather than doubled, producing a sophisticated aperitif-style drink that felt intentionally designed rather than accidentally compatible.

In other cocktail applications, I needed to exercise more care. The distinctive bitter profile can overpower delicate ingredients or clash with sweet components. Negronis worked surprisingly well, as the existing bitter elements in Campari found a complementary partner. Martinez-style drinks also benefited from the added complexity. However, in lighter serves like a French 75 or Aviation, I found the quininated notes too assertive, pulling focus from the intended flavor balance.

The pricing positions this as a premium, limited-edition expression, and I think that's appropriate marketing. This isn't an everyday gin for most drinkers—it's a specialized bottle that rewards specific applications and appeals to those who genuinely enjoy bitter flavors. I wouldn't recommend it as someone's only gin, but as an addition to a developed collection, it offers genuine variety.

The bottle design maintains Hendrick's distinctive aesthetic while clearly differentiating this expression. The presentation feels premium without being ostentatious, though I did find myself wishing for more detailed tasting notes on the label to guide first-time users toward optimal serving suggestions.

Ultimately, Orbium succeeds as an experimental expression that pushes beyond safe territory. It won't be everyone's preference, and I suspect many casual gin drinkers will find it too bitter or medicinal. But for those who appreciate amari, quinquinas, and aperitif-style spirits, this offers a compelling bridge between the gin category and those more bitter traditions. I've enjoyed having it in my collection for specific moods and occasions, even if it doesn't see as much rotation as more versatile bottles.
